The exercise sessions were a nightmare. Due to overlaps, I could only attend the Friday group. This would have been fine, but the rate at which the exercise problems were published made it impossible to prepare sufficiently for someone who wasn't fluent in Mathlab. At best there was 2 days time to get to know the material. One could argue that there was 45 minutes in the session to prepare, but the sessions themselves were so noisy and unrestful due to everyone talking and shouting over eachother that it was extremely difficult to even hear the teacher at times; concentrating on work was impossible, especially for someone slightly socially awkward. After the first couple sessions I fell behind and could not catch up during the whole rest of the course. The only thing that motivated me to even try was the threat of negative points on the exam, which turned even more questionable and stressful when the exercise scoring was retroactively changed 3/4ths through the course. In the end I learned nothing at all from the exercises themselves, it was an exercise in frustration and a constant struggle against Mathlab. Everything I learned was from the lectures or what little I managed to cram before the exam. This is exceedingly sad, because the way Joni laid out the whole ImageNet challenge and how we could do something with it felt very exciting to me at the beginning of the course. The exam itself was very frustrating too, requiring the exact memorization of algorithms that would be trivially looked up in any real world scenario for 8 out of 12 points worth of problems. I do not feel like the exam measured what the course description has as the objectives, it was supposed to be an introduction to pattern recognition and machine learning course, but the first problem was the only one that felt like it was structurally fitting for an introduction course. One algorithm usage problem would have felt alright, but requiring recalling two whole algorithms for 2/3rds of the points seems counterproductive and excessive. It does not measure whether the student understands where to apply these algorithms, only whether they can recall them by name. I could not recall the specific equations after reading 'cmeans' so, woe is me, 4 points down the drain... 1/3rd of the exam. I would improve the course by making the exercises completely optional with no penalty, adding sessions on more days or making submitting by email more viable. In any case make sure the exercise problems are published such that the FIRST group of the week has at least 5 days to prepare. Make the exercises more relevant by adding points for attendance or quiz style material like the first couple sessions had, to motivate students who aren't as strong with Matlab to even come to the session and try. The bottom line though is, making the course artificially harder to pass for someone who doesn't want to or can't work with the exercises stressful timeline feels like an unjust judgement. If the exercises truly have learning value to someone, not participating already makes the course more difficult for them. But then you get the double whammy of being slapped in the face with negative exam points for up to 1/3rd of the exam. Why didn't I speak out during the course? Because I was too busy trying to make things work for me.
